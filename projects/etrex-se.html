Creating a successor to Garmin's bestselling handheld GPS

Garmin's eTrex product line is known for being a reliable entry-level GPS with incredible battery life.

Last updated in 2011 with the eTrex 10, the device needed new hardware and a visual refresh to adapt it to the modern landscape.

Background

Without the ability to display maps, the main purpose of the eTrex is to track your location with a breadcrumb trail of where you've been. This tracking feature provides users with a way back in case they get lost.

Despite the widespread availability of mobile navigation apps, Garmin's handheld GPS devices occupy a niche aimed at users who need a reliable, rugged navigator with enough battery life to last throughout their adventure.

In the years since its release, the eTrex 10 established itself as Garmin's bestselling outdoor handheld. Due to its durability, small size, and entry-level price, it became hugely popular among geocachers and hikers.

The eTrex 10 has been known to survive falls down mountains, ski slopes, and rivers.

Problem

Major button changes
For the newest iteration of the eTrex, a business-level decision had been made to cut the number of button inputs in half to improve its durability and reduce manufacturing costs.

This required a complete redesign of the interface, which we took as an opportunity to update the product for a new generation of adventurers.

Without the thumbstick, users needed a completely different way to interact with the device.

Tracking Update

In addition to the button changes we needed to accommodate, we also wanted to improve the tracking experience and tighten up the convoluted menu system.

For all of Garmin's GPS products, tracking has several key components:

  • Displaying a trackline of where the user has been
  • Controls for saving, pausing, or resetting the tracks
  • Data such as distance, elevation, or time elapsed
  • Other common actions, like marking the current location or starting navigation

On the eTrex 10, all of these features were scattered across the device as individual menu items with no clear relation to one another.

Default menu setup on the eTrex 10

Design

After brainstorming several potential paths, we decided to combine all of the tracking-related features in a single "Tracking" app.

Sectioning off each tracking-related feature as different pages in the same space let us keep the map, tracking information, and compass all within reach of each other with minimal back-and-forth between other apps and menus.

Replacing the menu button

With the removal of the etrex 10's Menu button, we needed a new way to access additional actions and controls.

We adopted a top-right corner indicator that animates open to reveal the menu when OK is pressed. That way, all the user needs to do to access controls or start navigation is turn the device on and hit OK twice.

Making up for the lack of maps

Most of our products with black-and-white displays get better battery life, a more compact size, and entry-level pricing at the cost of the storage capacity and processing power required for mapping. Since the only thing we can show is the user's tracks of where they've been and any nearby waypoints that have been marked, the resulting 'map' screen tends to look empty and underwhelming .

I brainstormed and pitched a variety of solutions to the team, and eventually landed on the map grid. The grid would appear in the background behind all other onscreen elements and help create a sense of scale and direction in an otherwise empty space.

Results

We launched the eTrex SE on March 14, 2023 with the UX completed several months prior. This was followed up with the eTrex Solar, a solar-powered variant of the device that can run indefinitely in sunny conditions.

In total, I created over 40 flows to cover both products and built more than 330 components in the process.

Conclusion

The eTrex SE was the first product I was fully responsible for and served as a major learning experience that taught me many of the technical, organizational, and collaborative skills I would need to tackle future projects.

If there was one thing I'd like to do differently next time, it'd be more testing and external validation of the designs. Due to the size and structure of my team at the time, we didn't have the time or resources necessary to conduct testing as a part of the design process. I think there were many design decisions that I think could've been resolved faster, and areas of improvement we might have caught earlier with testing.

Overall, I really enjoyed working on the SE and am looking forward to seeing how users engage with it in the next few years.
