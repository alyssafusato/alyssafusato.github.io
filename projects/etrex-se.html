Creating a successor to Garmin's bestselling handheld

Timeline

18 months (2021 - 2023)

Project Role

Lead UX Designer

Garmin's eTrex product line is known for being a reliable entry-level GPS with incredible battery life. Last updated in 2011, the device needed new hardware and a visual refresh to adapt it to the modern landscape.

This was the first product I was fully responsible for and served as a major learning experience that taught me many of the technical, organizational, and collaborative skills I would need to tackle future projects. Through it, I had to adapt to a new design environment with vastly different technological constraints and be proactive in identifying areas for improvement while coming up with functional solutions.

Background

Despite the widespread availability of navigation apps, Garmin's handheld GPS devices occupy a niche aimed at users who need a reliable, rugged navigator with enough battery life to last throughout their adventure.

Without the ability to display maps, the main purpose of the eTrex is to display your location with a breadcrumb trail of where you've been. This tracking feature provides users with a way back in case they get lost.

The eTrex 10 has been known to survive falls down mountains, ski slopes, and rivers.

In the years since its release, the eTrex 10 established itself as Garmin's bestselling outdoor handheld. Due to its durability, small size, and entry-level price, it became hugely popular among geocachers and hikers.

Problem

For the newest iteration of the eTrex, a business-level decision had been made to cut the number of button inputs in half to improve its longevity and reduce manufacturing costs.

Without the thumbstick, users needed a whole new way to interact with the device.

This required a complete redesign of the interface, which we saw as an opportunity to update the product for a new generation of adventurers.

Our next biggest priority was to consolidate the eTrex 10’s large and unwieldy menu structure, which often forced users to go back and forth between various apps. It was a recurring complaint on the previous eTrex, especially since there were so many apps to choose from that were not always helpful or relevant to users.

Design

To make up for the reduced inputs, we had to make the most of each button. By being very intentional about each screen's layout we found ways to repurpose the "OK" button to serve multiple functions based on context.

One example is the menu, which used to have a dedicated button on the eTrex 10. When “OK” is used to open the menu instead of its usual action, we show a special indicator in the corner- right next to where the OK button is physically located on the device.

This affordance has the added benefit of making it easier to tell when you have extra actions to choose from on a given page.

On the 10, there is no way to tell if a screen has menu options- you have to press the Menu button to find out. On the SE, additional options are indicated by the top right Menu icon.

The eTrex 10’s structure resembled a toolbox with an array of discrete tools that were to be used individually as needed. This led to a lot of back-and-forth between pages and menus, since features that seemed related were sometimes located in completely different apps. It also put critical features on the same level as extremely niche ones, which was especially confusing for new users.

In response, we consolidated the most important features and grouped them into a single “Tracking” app that appears at the top of your list. This will be where the user spends the most time, and reduces the need to jump between various other apps.

In contrast to the flat, directionless structure of the eTrex 10 (left), we grouped related features together to provide a more guided experience on the eTrex SE (right).

As I worked on the eTrex, something that kept bothering me was the vast emptiness of the “map” screen. It felt extremely blank and aimless for one of our most important pages, especially as a part of a navigation and wayfinding device.

I brainstormed several solutions and pitched the map grid to the team. The gridlines would appear in the background to help create a sense of scale and direction in an otherwise contextless space. Originally the grid was square and matched the size of the scale bar, but we had to make a few compromises due to technical constraints.

Once we got to see a prototype of the grid in action, the team immediately wanted it for all of our non-mapping products moving forward.

Garmin's non-mapping products. The new grid on the eTrex SE helps orient the user in the absence of other cues.

Conclusion

We launched the eTrex SE on March 14, 2023 with the UX completed several months prior. This was followed up with the eTrex Solar, a solar-powered variant of the device with infinite battery life in sunny conditions.

In total, I created over 40 flows to cover both products and built more than 330 components in the process.

Due to differences in display types and screen resolutions, it’s difficult to make a core library for our handheld electronics. I learned a lot about building components as a result!

If there was one thing I'd like to do differently next time, it'd be more testing and external validation of the designs. We didn't have the resources or infrastructure to conduct testing as a part of our process back then, but I think it could've helped us make more informed design choices and discover areas of improvement earlier.

Overall, I really enjoyed working on the SE and am looking forward to seeing how users engage with it over the years. The device is like a small system unto itself, and being in charge of designing all of it was a fun and interesting challenge. While the experience may not seem as relevant to places focused on building apps and websites, it gave me the chance to make mistakes and gain skills that I carry with me to this day.
