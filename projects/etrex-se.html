Creating a successor to Garmin's bestselling handheld GPS

Garmin's eTrex product line is known for being a reliable entry-level GPS with incredible battery life.
Last updated in 2011, the device needed new hardware and a visual refresh to adapt it to the modern landscape.

First big boy project! I was put in charge of the eTrex SE and its upgraded variant, the eTrex Solar.

This project challenged me to mediate between historical precedent and innovation. Designing for consumer electronics instead of mobile or web also presented a completely novel set of rules and constraints to work with.

Background

Despite the widespread availability of navigation apps, Garmin's handheld GPS devices occupy a niche aimed at users who need a reliable, rugged navigator with enough battery life to last throughout their adventure.

Without the ability to display maps, the main purpose of the eTrex is to track your location with a breadcrumb trail of where you've been. This tracking feature provides users with a way back in case they get lost.

In the years since its release, the eTrex 10 established itself as Garmin's bestselling outdoor handheld. Due to its durability, small size, and entry-level price, it became hugely popular among geocachers and hikers.

The eTrex 10 has been known to survive falls down mountains, ski slopes, and rivers.

For the newest iteration of the eTrex, a business-level decision had been made to cut the number of button inputs in half to improve its durability and reduce manufacturing costs.

This required a complete redesign of the interface, which we saw as an opportunity to update the product for a new generation of adventurers.

Without the thumbstick, users needed a completely different way to interact with the device.

Trouble with Tracking

probably cut this

In addition to the button changes we needed to accommodate, we also wanted to improve the tracking experience and overhaul the convoluted menu system.

For Garmin's outdoor GPS devices, tracking has four key components:

  • Displaying a trackline of where the user has been
  • Controls for saving, pausing, or resetting the tracks
  • Data such as distance, elevation, or time elapsed
  • Other common actions, like marking the current location or starting navigation

On the eTrex 10, all of these features were scattered across the device as individual menu items with no clear relation to one another.

The eTrex 10's confusing menu system has spawned many online guides, tutorials, and walkthrough videos.

Design

Unifying the tracking experience

card format w problem -> solutions

First, we combined all of the tracking-related features into a single app that captures the core experience of the device.

By turning each tracking-related feature into pages grouped under the same umbrella, we were able to have the map, tracking information, and compass all within reach of each other.

We also put the most frequently used actions in the menu instead of forcing the user to leave the app to access them.

Replacing the menu button

With the removal of the Menu button, we needed a new way to access additional actions and controls on every screen with additional options.

We adopted a top-right corner indicator that animates open to reveal the menu when the "OK" button is pressed. That way, all the user needs to do to access controls or start navigation is turn on the device and hit OK twice.

By being very intentional about each screen's layout, we were able to repurpose the "OK" button to serve multiple functions based on context.

Adding the map grid

Something that bothered me endlessly about the eTrex SE was the map, or lack thereof.

Since the only things on it are the user's tracks and any nearby waypoints, the resulting screen tends to look empty and underwhelming.

This was the accepted status quo for all of Garmin's non-mapping products, but I was determined to find a way for the eTrex SE to stand apart.

Garmin's non-mapping products. The new grid on the eTrex SE helps orient the user in the absence of other cues.

One of the solutions I pitched to the team was the map grid. The gridlines would appear in the background behind all other onscreen elements and help create a sense of scale and direction in an otherwise contextless space.

Once we got to see a prototype of the feature in action, the team immediately wanted it for all of our non-mapping products moving forward.

Results

we will not even try to make this its own section. combine with last section

We launched the eTrex SE on March 14, 2023 with the UX completed several months prior. This was followed up with the eTrex Solar, a solar-powered variant of the device that can run indefinitely in sunny conditions.

In total, I created over 40 flows to cover both products and built more than 330 components in the process.

Conclusion

The eTrex SE was the first product I was fully responsible for and served as a major learning experience that taught me many of the technical, organizational, and collaborative skills I would need to tackle future projects.

If there was one thing I'd like to do differently next time, it'd be more testing and external validation of the designs. We didn't have the resources or infrustructure to conduct testing as a part of our process at the time, but I think it could've helped us make more informed design choices and discover areas of improvement.

I really enjoyed working on the SE and am looking forward to seeing how users engage with it over the years.
