Co-authoring Garmin's first wireframing kit for wearables

Wearables are a major part of Garmin’s device portfolio, boasting 6+ regularly updated product families and making up nearly 40% of the company’s total revenue.

Before this project, wearables designers on the UX team had no dedicated resources for building wireframes. They often used elements pasted from older files, edited screenshots, or created their own components, resulting in frequent inconsistencies and obsolete design patterns.

Timeline
12 months, Sep 2024-2025
Role
Main library architect
Collaborators
UX Lead

Discovery

Exploring the problem space & understanding the vision

We kicked things off by studying other design systems and learning about the day-to-day problems encountered by the wearables team.

During this phase, we wanted to learn more about:

  • The issues the team wanted the kit would address
  • How they imagined the kit would integrate into their workflow
  • Any concerns or anxieties they had about the kit’s potential impact on the team

The core issue boiled down to two things:

Each designer had to independently figure out how to rebuild common elements, interactions, and flows.
This, compounded by a lack of documentation or standardization for our wearables UI, meant that mocking up simple screens and flows could become extremely time consuming and laborious. Which led to the next issue...
To keep up, designers often resorted to using time-saving shortcuts.
Some strategies included:
  • Copying elements from existing files
  • Making their own ad hoc components
  • Editing screenshots to use as stand-ins for less important elements

Cascading pain points

Outdated designs circulated quickly & easily
Since designers often copied from existing documents to save time, obsolete interactions weresometimes perpetuated across multiple projects.
Difficulty with onboarding & collaboration
Made onboarding much slower for the rapidly-growing wearables team. Handoff between designers became much more involved since everyone mocks up screens their own way.
Slow feature conversions
Converting features from one product family to another was a very common task that took an inordinate amount of time to complete, especially if the designer is not familiar with one or both product families.
Hard to tell if reused UI elements were reliable, very time-consuming to validate
Documentation on the current/final implementation for each product was often lacking, so validating designs typically involved obtaining an updated device and recreating the interaction.
Redundancies & inconsistencies between products
Documentation on the current/final implementation for each product was often lacking, so validating designs typically involved obtaining an updated device and recreating the interaction.
With all of these problems, why did the team put off building a library for so long?

Concerns about establishing a kit

In spite of the situation, the team also had a lot of anxieties about how having a library could change the way the team works.
A major concern was around the loss of creative freedom and innovation, which are defining values for the Wearables team. Such concerns have prevented us from investing in design systems and libraries in the past, and were something we wanted to familiarize ourselves with before moving forward.
Systems are often resistant to change
Unresponsive to feedback, stifles creative freedom and exploration, very difficult for users to provide input. “We invested in the library so it’s better to just default to using it rather than try new things.”
Resource-heavy
Figma libraries require a huge amount of work to build and maintain, on top of the product ecosystem knowledge and system architecture skills needed to even start.
Slow to update
Team cannot afford to wait for months for component requests to get processed and handled, designers will just employ a shortcut and move on.
Difficult to understand and use
Users do not really understand how it works, can’t get it to do what they want, or can’t find what they need. Leads to frequent detaching or creating own components. It can be difficult to find what you need and it takes time to learn how elements are meant to be used.
Disconnected from users
by having the library team operate separately from the designers using the kit, library gradually becomes less in touch with the needs of designers and communication will deteriorate over time as the library becomes an end in itself.

Strategy

Charting a path forward

We needed to find a way to build this kit with limited resources and tenuous buy-in.

Due to the unique needs of the wearables ecosystem compared to something more standardized like mobile or web, we were also working with a completely foreign set of parameters and considerations.

To ensure the kit’s success, we developed several key principles to keep us grounded as we began building.

Guiding Principles

Quick Start

Focus on building the most common elements first, test, & rapidly iterate
{{ "Rationale" | upcase }}
Figure out what works quickly, avoid decision paralysis and perfectionism

Maintainability

Prioritize structural simplicity, consistency, and clarity
{{ "Rationale" | upcase }}
Creates a stable, learnable system that can be passed onto new designers and architects

Easy Adoption

Design for convenience, ease-of-use, and findability
{{ "Rationale" | upcase }}
Encourages widespread adoption and overall buy-in from both users and leadership

Stay Connected

Regular rhythm for feedback & updates, maintain a strong relationship with end users
{{ "Rationale" | upcase }}
Helps the kit stay relevant and creates a friendly collaborative environment that allows for honest feedback
