Designing a home for Garmin archery & ballistics
Timeline 21 months (2022 - 2023)
Project Role Lead UX Designer

Summary

ShotView is a mobile platform for managing Garmin archery and ballistics devices, starting with our upcoming radar chronograph. As the sole designer on the project, I was in charge of bringing the app from a few rough concept screens into a fully-realized app experience.

Users record shots on the C1 Pro Chronograph, which are sent to the ShotView app for easier viewing and management.

Created to launch alongside the C1 Pro chronograph, the ShotView app needed to allow users to view, edit, and export shot data recorded by the C1 device. In the future, ShotView will also have to support many other Garmin archery and ballistics products, requiring a highly adaptive and scalable design.

What is the C1 Pro Chronograph?

Chronographs are devices that measure projectile speeds. They are often used by hunters and long-range shooters to optimize their gear for peak accuracy and consistency.

Most chronographs needed massive shades, a dedicated stand, bulky carrying cases, or lengthy calibration procedures. By contrast, the Garmin C1 Pro’s ultra compact size and effortless setup process made the device unlike anything before it.

The C1 Pro is small enough to fit in your pocket without compromising on accuracy or reliability.

Consumer electronics like the C1 Pro will often pair to a mobile app for additional functionality and support. I designed the ShotView app to have several additional features to further enhance the C1 experience, but they were ultimately shelved in a late-project scope change.

The app’s main focus shifted to allow users to review, edit, and share the shot data recorded on the C1 Pro.

A clear & informative review screen

The review screen for each session needed to show aggregated values for the larger activity along with specific metrics for individual shots.

I leaned into visual hierarchy to draw out the most important stats up front while still providing access to individual shot details below. Here’s how ShotView’s review screen stacks up to our competitors:

The review screens on other chronograph apps have nearly idential information and mainly differ in presentation

I built the review screen using modular sections that allow the layout to be easily added onto or rearranged to suit our needs. This became crucial later into development when features had to be cut for time. Instead of being forced to refactor everything, all I needed to do was take a few sections out for launch so they could be re-added in a later release.

A breakdown of the Session Review screen, which has been organized into modular sections.

Building with future growth in mind

On top of gradually introducing major feature updates over time, ShotView would need to support upcoming archery and ballistics devices with completely different needs and use cases.

This required a flexible design that could accommodate new features and functionality without completely disrupting the user’s prior experience.

Main tabs have a consistent structure while the device and review pages are tailored to suit the needs of each product.

Organizing the app this way means that new archery and ballistics devices can pair to ShotView instead of needing an entirely new companion app.

Adding flexibility to the home screen

The Home screen is made up of cards and button elements that can adapt to whichever devices are currently paired to the app. As ShotView grows, the top section of Home can be used to display insights, analytics, or shortcuts to common actions.

I designed the session cards on the home screen with new future session types in mind. There are several new types on our roadmap that use this card as a template with adjustments tailored to their content.

Bringing app improvements into the device

Ideally, users would pair the ShotView app and the C1 chronograph for the best possible experience, but there are some users who prefer to use the C1 on its own. To accommodate these users, I pulled in UI updates to mirror the app's design.

The C1’s much smaller screen size, limited fonts, and black-and-white pixel display meant that UI elements had to be adjusted in order to work. Below are some changes that make it easier to identify sessions at a glance and help reduce the amount of button presses needed to see relevant information.

These improvements help reduce cognitive load when the user is browsing recent sessions on the device.

Feedback-driven additions

There were a few new suggestions for improvements that I investigated and implemented after launch. One of them was the ability to bulk select sessions for export, which was a major perk for users who were recording lots of sessions and wanted to share them for further analysis.

Conclusion

The C1 Pro and ShotView released together on October 19, 2023 to great success, prompting the C1 to go out of stock for months afterward.

Garmin’s sales team had underestimated the units needed by about tenfold, eliciting this reaction from a user on a popular rifle sports forum.

While external limitations prevented the team from implementing everything that had been planned, the need to adapt rapidly within those constraints helped me rethink the way I approach design projects. Serving as the UX lead on this product taught me to trust my intuition as a designer, and it was satisfying to see the tangible results of the whole team's contributions.

ShotView was a rare opportunity for me to design an app from the ground up and experience all of the ups and downs associated with the full process. I'm proud of have been a part of the project and am excited to see where it goes in the future.
