Designing a home for XERO ballistics

Garmin's XERO product family encapsulates the hunting, archery, and rifle sports categories.
Since its debut in 2018, XERO had been rapidly growing and was now confident it had a product that would change the game forever.

The XERO C1 Pro is a radar chronograph, which measures and records bullet speeds. I was put in charge of the design for the C1 and its companion app ShotView in early 2022.

While the device was fairly minimal and basically complete, the app was still in its concepting stage and desperately needed an overhaul.

Background

Chronographs are widely used for load development, which is the process of fine-tuning the best combination of ammunition components (bullet, case, powder, primer, etc) for maximum accuracy and consistency. Since anything from wind conditions to the temperature of the firearm's barrel Earth's spin can alter a bullet's trajectory, serious hunters and long-range shooters are highly invested in tracking the performance of their gear.

The team was working on a new ultra-compact chronograph, the XERO C1 Pro.

I was brought onto the project to help with refinements on the device and to design a new companion app that would sync data from the C1 for easier viewing and sharing.

The size of the C1 Pro relative to other popular chronographs

Research

Knowing basically nothing about the hunting and sports shooting scene, I interviewed several shooters and did a competitive analysis of existing chronograph apps to get a better understanding of what users wanted to see.

By looking at the features they offered and how they presented different kinds of information, I was able to get a sense for what kind of information was important to users.

Screenshots from the Caldwell Precision Chronograph and FX Radar mobile apps

Design

Due to unexpected time, resouce, and technical constraints, we realized that we had to cut many of the app features we had already designed for launch.

Many core elements of the app had to be changed in order to make sense in the absence of certain key features.

This drastically changed the focus of the app, which became more about finding ways to present session stats without being able to use charts or other complex visuals.
Several rejected iterations of the main data section

Main screens

The result is a minimalistic app with a strong emphasis on visual hierarchy to showcase session stats.

These screens form the basis of the app experience until we have an opportunity to add more of the advanced features we've designed.

App Overview

A simplified map of ShotView's main screens is diagrammed below. The app's shallow architecture serves as a simple modular base for future additions, whether for other ballistics devices or new chronograph-related features.

Device Improvements

While users would ideally get ShotView for the best possible experience, we knew that they'd be reluctant to install a new app unless absolutely necessary. To create a better experience for these users, I pulled in some UI updates based on the app's design.

Feedback & Results

The C1 Pro and ShotView released on October 19, 2023.

Reception was overwhelmingly positive for the device and it became imfamously backordered for at least several months after launch.

Manufacturing struggled to keep up with surging demand since our sales team had underestimated the units we'd need by about tenfold.

I'll find an image for this later

Reception to the ShotView app was more mixed

Some users were happy with the app's detailed session view and note-taking tools, while others were hoping for more advanced features.

These included location-based weather data, the ability to create weapon and projectile profiles associated with each session, a live view of the current session, and the ability to add photos.

Nearly all of these were identified and designed for early on, but couldn't be included due to scope changes later in development. Ultimately, I think the feedback provides a solid basis for ShotView's future growth.

The general tone of some of these reactions makes me wonder if the hype surrounding the device led to equally high expectations for the app.

We also found several helpful suggestions for improvements from customers that we investigated and implemented. One standout was the addition of bulk exporting for sessions, which was a major plus for users who had their own systems for tracking and processing the data they recorded.

Wireframe flow for bulk exporting sessions

Conclusion

Working on ShotView was as a rare opportunity for me to design an app from the ground up and experience all of the ups and downs associated with it.

While external limitations prevented us from implementing everything we'd planned, learning how to work within those constraints to improvise solutions has helped me rethink the way I approach design projects.

Being the sole UX contact on ShotView taught me to trust more in my intuition as a designer, and it was really satisfying to see the tangible results of everyone's contributions.

Overall, ShotView has been a very unique project with huge potential for growth, and I'm excited to see where it goes in the future.
