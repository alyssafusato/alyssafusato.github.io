Designing a home for XERO ballistics

Garmin's XERO product family encapsulates the hunting, archery, and rifle sports categories.
Following its 2018 debut, XERO had seen rapid growth and established itself as a product poised to change the game forever.

The XERO C1 Pro is a radar chronograph, a device that measures and records bullet speeds. In early 2022, I came onboard as the UX lead for the C1 and its companion app ShotView. The conceptual stage of the device was nearing completion, but the companion app was stuck in its initial stages and desperately in need of a fresh start.

While the device was fairly minimal and basically complete, the app was still in its concepting stage and desperately needed an overhaul.

Background

In load development, chronographs are used to determine the ideal mix of ammunition components—bullet, case, powder, and primer—to achieve the best possible accuracy and consistency. Many external factors can affect a bullet's trajectory, from wind and barrel temperature to even the Earth’s rotation, so dedicated hunters and long-range marksmen rely on chronographs to understand and optimize their gear’s performance.

The team was working on a new ultra-compact chronograph, the XERO C1 Pro.
The size of the C1 Pro relative to other popular chronographs

Research

I had no background in hunting and sports shooting prior to this project. To gain familiarity with this space, I conducted interviews with several shooters and wrote a competitive analysis of existing chronograph apps.

Through this process, I gained a sense of current design patterns, user expectations, and opportunities for Garmin to set itself apart in the market.

Screenshots from the Caldwell Precision Chronograph and FX Radar mobile apps

Design

Due to unexpected constraints, many of the designed app features were cut prior to launch. The elements of the app that remained required changes to compensate for the loss of key features, and this drastically changed the focus of the app.

ShotView pivoted to prioritize presentation of session stats in a way that would still be informative and insightful for users.
Several rejected iterations of the main data section

Main screens

The result is a minimalistic app with a strong emphasis on visual hierarchy to showcase session stats.

App Overview

A simplified map of ShotView's main screens is diagrammed below. The app's shallow, modular architecture serves as a scalable base so new features can easily be added in, whether for other ballistics devices or new chronograph features.

Device Improvements

Ideally, users would pair the ShotView app and the C1 chronograph for the best possible experience, but there are some users who prefer to use C1 on its own. To accommodate these users, I pulled in UI updates to mirror the app's design.

Feedback & Results

The C1 Pro and ShotView released on October 19, 2023.

Reception was overwhelmingly positive for the device and it became imfamously backordered for at least several months after launch.

Manufacturing struggled to keep up with surging demand since Garmin's sales team had underestimated the units needed by about tenfold.

Found on a forum about the C1 (I did not make this)

Reception to the ShotView app was mixed

Some users were happy with the app's detailed session view and note-taking tools, while others were hoping for more advanced features.

Commonly requested features include location-based weather data, photos, weapon and projectile profiles, and a live view of the current session.

Nearly all of these features were identified and designed early in the development process but could not be included due to scope changes. The feedback provided is encouraging and confirms the team's vision of how to serve users in the future.

The general tone of some of these reactions makes me wonder if the hype surrounding the device led to equally high expectations for the app.

There were also a few new suggestions for improvements that were investigated and implemented after launch. One standout was the addition of bulk exports for sessions, which was a major perk for users who had their own external systems for tracking and processing the data they recorded.

Wireframe flow for bulk exporting sessions

Conclusion

ShotView was a rare opportunity for me to design an app from the ground up and experience all of the ups and downs associated with the full process.

While external limitations prevented the team from implementing everything that had been planned, the need to adapt rapidly within those constraints made me rethink the way I approach design projects.

Serving as the lead designer on ShotView taught me to trust my intuition more as a designer, and it was satisfying to see the tangible results of the whole team's contributions.

Overall, ShotView is a unique product with huge potential for growth, I'm proud of have been a part of it, and I'm excited to see where it goes in the future.
